The sources provide a comprehensive overview of evaluation metrics and methodologies across different machine reasoning tasks, including supervised learning, clustering, recommender systems, and search techniques.

### **1. Supervised Learning Evaluation**
The files distinguish between metrics for classification (categorical outcomes) and regression (continuous values).

**Classification Metrics:**
*   **Confusion Matrix:** A primary tool used to display the correct and incorrect rates of a classifier for different classes.
*   **Accuracy:** Calculated as $(TP + TN) / \text{Total } P+N$; it measures the overall correctness but may not reflect performance on imbalanced data.
*   **Precision and Recall:** Precision ($TP / (TP+FP)$) measures accuracy among positive predictions, while Recall ($TP / (TP+FN)$) measures the ability to find all positive instances.
*   **F1 Score (F-measure):** Defined as the **harmonic mean of precision and recall**, providing a single score that balances both.
*   **AUC (Area Under the Curve) & ROC Curve:** The ROC curve plots the true positive rate (TPR) against the false positive rate (FPR). **AUC** summarizes this performance across all possible thresholds, representing the probability that a model ranks a random positive instance higher than a negative one.
*   **MCC (Matthews Correlation Coefficient):** Noted as more reliable than accuracy or F1 score for **imbalanced datasets** because it considers all four values of the confusion matrix.

**Regression Metrics:**
*   **MSE (Mean Squared Error):** A common measure of model accuracy that calculates the average squared difference between predicted and actual values.
*   **RMSE (Root Mean Square Error):** The square root of MSE, used to measure prediction error magnitude.
*   **MAE (Mean Absolute Error):** Measures the average absolute difference between predictions and actual values.
*   **$R^2$ (Coefficient of Determination):** Identified as the **most used score for regression performance** because it accounts for data variation by comparing model error to a simple average.

### **2. Recommender System Evaluation**
Evaluation for recommender systems covers both the accuracy of rating predictions and the relevance of ranked item lists.

**Ranking Metrics:**
*   **Precision at K (P@K):** The proportion of recommended items in the top-K set that are relevant (e.g., items the user is known to like with a rating $\ge 4$).
*   **AP@K (Average Precision at K):** The mean of $P@i$ for $i=1, \dots, K$, typically calculated for a single user.
*   **MAP@K (Mean Average Precision at K):** The mean of the AP@K scores across all users in the system.

**Rating and Similarity Metrics:**
*   **MAE and RMSE:** Used to test the accuracy of predicted ratings against known values in a test set.
*   **Similarity Measures:** Used to rank unrated items, including **Cosine Similarity**, **Euclidean Distance**, **Pearson Correlation**, and **Jaccard Similarity** (for binary/set-based data).

---

### **3. Clustering and Unsupervised Evaluation**
Evaluation in clustering focuses on the internal structure of the formed groups.
*   **Silhouette Coefficient:** A measure of both **cohesion** (how close elements are within a cluster) and **separation** (how far clusters are from each other). A higher score generally indicates better clustering.
*   **Dispersion:** Measured by the sum of distances (or squared Euclidean distances) of records from their cluster centroid.
*   **Qualitative Review:** Validating clusters through **interpretability** (human examination) and **stability** (testing if clusters remain consistent when the data is partitioned).

---

### **4. Search, Optimization, and Knowledge Graphs**
*   **Informed Search:** Uses an **evaluation function $f(n)$**, often incorporating a **heuristic function $h(n)$**, which estimates the cost from node $n$ to the goal. A heuristic of $0$ indicates the goal has been reached.
*   **Knowledge Graph Embeddings:** Models like **TransE** use a **score function** $r(h, t) = -\|h+r-t\|$ to evaluate triples; lower values indicate a better representation of the relationship.
*   **Reinforcement Learning:** Evaluated via **reward functions** and **Q functions**.

---

### **5. General Validation Methodologies**
*   **Data Splitting:** The standard practice is randomly dividing data into **training sets** (e.g., 75%) and **test sets** (e.g., 25%).
*   **Empirical Risk:** Defined as the measured error rate specifically on the training set.
*   **Cross-Validation:** 
    *   **LOOCV (Leave-One-Out):** Training on $n-1$ samples and validating on $1$, repeated $n$ times.
    *   **k-fold:** Partitioning data into $k$ folds, training on $k-1$ and testing on $1$, then averaging the results to estimate the validation error rate.
